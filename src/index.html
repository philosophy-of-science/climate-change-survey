Philosophy of Science Association

Climate Change Survey Results

A Report of the Climate Change Task Force

Results

During March 2021, the Philosophy of Science Association Climate Task Force (CTF) issued a survey aimed at gathering information on members” experiences of working in a more online environment as a result of the Coronavirus crisis, and gauging attitudes towards continuing with some of these practices in the service of the climate crisis. Many other learned societies are currently asking similar questions. The full results of this survey are now available via the members” area of the website.

In the end almost 200 (189) of the PSA”s roughly 700 current members took the survey. Overall the results show a marked desire among the membership for a substantial decarbonization of the discipline. In particular:

  • Over half of respondents (57%) said that, given IPCC goals, they would like to reduce the carbon footprint associated with their research activity by 50% or more. A further 28% were interested in reducing it to some degree.
  • Over half of respondents (51%) said that, given IPCC goals, they would like to see many conferences and workshops continue to be hosted online.

Respondents were however less sure about whether online conferences should be the “default” for conferences involving geographically remote participants, with the move to in-person only when there is “good reason”: the result here was a pretty even split between “Yes”, “No”, and “Unsure.” As such, it seems clear that as a community there is considerable enthusiasm for moving to a more online model, but less clarity on the circumstances in which one model should be chosen over another.

Noteworthy also was that a sizeable majority of respondents (68%) agreed with the statement that “even if many conferences and workshops should continue to be hosted online, there is special reason to host the PSA biennial meeting remotely.” This obviously raises the question of how the PSA itself can reduce the carbon burden imposed by its activities. It also raises the question of how the PSA meeting might be re-envisioned to better serve the role of bringing people together in-person when there is less of that going on overall.

Summing up, it seems that many in our community envision us going forward roughly as follows: the PSA biennial meeting will be maintained as at least partly in-person, while smaller meetings will largely, although not wholly, be conducted online in the interests of decreasing our collective carbon footprint. We would like those who organize conferences and workshops to be aware that this is how many members of our community wish to proceed.

Problems with Online Meetings

While the reported quantitative data shows broad support for a more online conference model – the PSA meeting aside – qualitative survey responses make clear that some regard the online model as a very pale imitation of in-person meetings. And as may be seen from the answers to Question 2 and 6, it was the social aspect of these meetings together with “Zoom fatigue” that were seen as prime respects in which they fell short. Some respondents also enumerated ways in which online meetings did not always better serve those with care obligations or accessibility constraints. While some of the problems posed by online meetings might be reduced in a post-pandemic setting (given that e.g. office space will again be available to many of us and overall we will be on Zoom less), it is clear nevertheless that moving to a much more online model will discriminate against some in our community at the same time as it helps others. This obviously poses a dilemma given widespread support for making significant cuts to our carbon consumption that the community needs to address. As such, it seems that we as a community should carefully reflect upon questions such as:

  1. which situations warrant a reversion to carbon costly forms of meeting;
  2. how professional relationships between geographically distant scholars can be instigated and fostered without physical travel, particularly in the case of early-career scholars;
  3. how both sorts of meetings might be combined so as to achieve something of the “best of both worlds”.

Regarding the latter question, in addition to the familiar (and sometimes prohibitively expensive) “hybrid” model, some researchers have been working with a “hub” model in which multiple groups of scholars convene locally and then interact with distant such groups online (see for example these biologists). This is a model that philosophers of science may wish to experiment with. In the near future, the CTF and our partner organization Philosophers for Sustainability will be sharing resources from other learned societies reflecting on their own experiments in this area. Input from members on this issue is of course welcome.

Controversies Surrounding Effectiveness

It should be noted finally that some respondents felt that the focus on academic travel was misguided, and one that ran the risk of diverting attention away from other areas where we might more purposefully direct efforts against climate change. Some expressed the worry that these sorts of “individualistic” actions might actively undermine efforts to promote more systematic change, and saw the survey as a wasted opportunity to consider what other sorts of actions we could take as a society. While it is well-established that conference travel comprises a very significant portion of research-related carbon emissions (by UBC's measure, somewhere between 63 and 73% of the total associated with all campus operations), the question of the effectiveness of these sorts of interventions is a complicated one lacking any uncontroversial responses. Suffice to say here that for all these controversies, the survey results make clear that a majority of respondents do, as a matter of fact, want to significantly cut their research-related carbon footprint. While acknowledging that not everyone feels the same way, we take it those wishes ought to be respected by all in our community, and ideally in a way that minimizes the professional harms suffered by those who wish to travel less. As such, even those who have no such intention should consider how they can further the interests of those who do, such as by advocating for facilities for remote participation at the conferences that they organize.

These issues, together with other ways that the PSA can help in the climate fight, will be the topic of the President”s Forum at the 2021 meeting in Baltimore. The CTF would like to thank everyone who contributed to the survey. Special thanks also go to PSA Associate Caitlin Powell for handling the survey and for packaging the results.
